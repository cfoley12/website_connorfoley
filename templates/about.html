About Us

Connor Foley John Holmes Ross Pendergast Danny Sack

As four board members on the Michigan Running Club, to call running a “passion” of ours would be quite the understatement. Between running every day, poring over race results from teams around the country, and doing our own racing, running takes up a lot of our collective time. Running at the club/collegiate level is usually broken down into two main seasons: track and cross country. Comparing track results is fairly easy, as every track race is run on a flat, oval, two to four hundred meter surface, depending on if it’s indoor or outdoor season. Cross country results are a bit more fickle. Between courses of varying lengths, differing elevation changes ( read: hills), and weather making the gravel paths, grass loops, and backcountry trails we run on a bit wetter than we’d like, comparing results in Cross Country season is virtually impossible. The collection of conditions that contribute to a course’s general difficulty results in a wide variation of outcomes for competitors of possibly equivalent fitness and ability. This has long been a point of contention for Runners, who always argue whatever course they ran was tougher, and as a result, their time was more impressive. These arguments are generally non productive, and result in everyone wishing there was a way to normalize results. This mode of thinking came to a height when our Club Team, last year’s National Runner Ups, were ranked far lower than we would have expected in a series of season rankings released by our national governing body. We began to wonder if a way to compare these results existed, and if there was a way to make that work. And thus, our idea for a final project was born.
